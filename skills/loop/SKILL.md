# THE LOOP v3

The Conceptor/Director Loop is the creative engine of every project. It
replaces the traditional copywriter/art director team with a formalised
argument between language and image — between what the idea *is* and how
the idea *lands*.

In Omar's workflow, he holds both roles. But the loop must be externalised
and given genuine friction. A single voice agreeing with itself produces
mediocrity. Two voices in productive tension produce work.

---

## THE LINEAGE

**Bernbach (1960s):** Art Director + Copywriter. Two distinct minds with
different instincts forced into a room together. The tension between
visual thinking and verbal thinking produces work neither could make
alone. DDB built the Creative Revolution on this.

**PJ Pereira (Silverside AI):** The modern AI-enabled agency no longer
runs on copywriters and art directors. It runs on Conceptors and
Directors in a loop. The original art/copy team was a creative argument
made permanent. This maps onto the writer/director tradition in film.

**Goodfellow (2014):** Generative Adversarial Networks. Generator +
Discriminator. The same structural bet encoded in mathematics — quality
emerges from productive friction between two roles with different
mandates, not from one mind trying to do both.

**The Loop:** Bernbach's insight, formalised as a protocol. The Conceptor
is the generator. The Director is the discriminator. The loop is the
adversarial training cycle. Quality emerges from structured opposition,
not from a single pass.

## WHAT CHANGED IN V3

V2 introduced three speeds, extractable rules, and accident protection.
V3 adds the discipline of the discriminator — making the Director side
more structured, more quantified, and more grounded in evidence.

**Four changes:**

1. **Scoring dimensions.** The Director no longer critiques qualitatively
   alone. At full protocol speed, the Director scores work against
   explicit dimensions drawn from the Idea Storm's structural signatures.

2. **Feedback gradient.** The Director's response is not "try again" but
   a structured pass-back: which dimensions held, which fell short, and
   a directional note on each. The Conceptor gets a map, not a rejection.

3. **Convergence detection.** V2 capped the loop at three exchanges. V3
   makes termination quality-driven: the loop ends when the Director
   cannot meaningfully improve the work — when the score stabilises or
   the Conceptor's output matches the reference benchmark.

4. **Reference benchmarks.** The Idea Storm's 20 archetypes — each with
   a structural signature, failure mode, and exemplars — serve as the
   ground truth the Director evaluates against. The discriminator has
   training data.

**Everything from v2 is retained.** Three speeds, extractable rules,
accident protection, adapted roles, the Producer. V3 adds a layer of
discipline on top of the existing architecture. It does not replace it.

## THE THREE SPEEDS

### Stamp

The lightest loop. Both roles check and approve in a single gesture.

**When to use:** The decision is clear, the argument is served, and both
roles recognise it immediately. The work does not need friction — it needs
confirmation.

**What it sounds like:** "LOOP approved." Two words. No exchange. Both
roles are satisfied. The decision is logged but not debated.

**When NOT to use:** If either role hesitates, even slightly, the stamp
is not appropriate. Hesitation means there is friction to surface. Move
to working speed.

**What to document:** The decision and the stamp. One line.
Example: "Mobile portrait crop — LOOP approved."

**V3 note:** No scoring at stamp speed. Stamps are pre-convergence —
the work is already there.

### Working Speed

The standard loop for production decisions. One exchange, sometimes two.
The Conceptor states the argument. The Director tests it against execution.
A decision is reached. Rules may be extracted.

**When to use:** The decision involves a genuine creative choice — not
just confirmation — but the argument and execution are close enough that
a full protocol would be overhead.

**What it sounds like:**
- Conceptor: "The background is narrative — a specific person doing a
  specific thing. White type. The background does the argument."
- Director: "Confirmed. background-clip: text would compete with the
  image. Plain white lets the image carry the weight."
- Decision locked. Rule extracted: narrative backgrounds → white type.

**What to document:** The decision, the reasoning from both roles, and
any rule extracted.

**V3 note:** The Director may cite a scoring dimension informally at
working speed ("Emotional Authenticity is high, no notes") but full
scoring is not required. Working speed stays lean.

### Full Protocol

The heavyweight loop. Formal scoring. Feedback gradient. Convergence
detection.

**When to use:**
- The concept itself is being evaluated, not just a production decision
- The Conceptor and Director genuinely disagree
- The decision will set a precedent that governs the rest of the project
- The work is at a phase gate (concept lock, script lock, visual lock)

**What it sounds like:** See "The Full Protocol" below.

## THE FULL PROTOCOL

### Step 1: Conceptor Opens

The Conceptor states:
- The core argument in one sentence.
- The structural mechanism (the cut, the juxtaposition, the gap the
  audience closes).
- What every element of the current work must serve.
- Any conceptual concerns, weaknesses, or unresolved problems.
- **The Idea Storm archetype, if applicable.** Naming the archetype
  gives the Director an explicit reference benchmark to evaluate against.

**The Conceptor's opening must be specific.** "The concept is strong" is
not a Conceptor opening. "The concept is: the viewer is inside the
BROUHAHAHA before the word arrives, and the structural mechanism is a
crowd-surf photograph where one person is held up by many — joy as a
physical act, not a sentiment. Archetype: Metaphors Made Real" — that
is a Conceptor opening.

### Step 2: Director Scores

The Director translates the Conceptor's argument into execution language
and evaluates against the scoring dimensions.

**The five scoring dimensions:**

| Dimension | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **Argument Strength** | Does the work serve the core insight? Could the concept be removed and nothing would change? Or is it load-bearing? |
| **Structural Integrity** | Does the mechanism work? If an archetype is named, does the work match its structural signature? Does it avoid the archetype's failure mode? |
| **Emotional Authenticity** | Does this feel real? Would a human being respond to this, or does it read as a creative exercise? |
| **Execution Survivability** | Can this be made? Does it survive contact with the medium, the platform, the format, the budget? |
| **Distinctiveness** | Is this the zig or the zag? If you showed this to the category, would they recognise it as different — or as another version of what they already do? |

**How scoring works:**

The Director does not assign numbers. The Director assigns one of three
states to each dimension:

- **Holds** — this dimension is working. No notes.
- **Soft** — this dimension has potential but is not yet earning its place.
  Directional note required: what specifically would strengthen it.
- **Fails** — this dimension is broken. The work cannot proceed until
  this is addressed. Specific diagnosis required.

**The Director's response must be grounded in craft and in the score.**
"I think the visuals should be more dynamic" is not a Director's note.
"Structural Integrity: SOFT. The mechanism is a genre hijack but the
genre conventions are not established clearly enough for the subversion
to land. The audience needs 15 seconds of pure genre before the brand
enters, not 5. Execution Survivability: HOLDS. This can be made in the
format." — that is a Director's note.

### Step 3: Feedback Gradient

This is the v3 addition that changes the exchange. The Director does not
just evaluate — the Director provides a gradient: which dimensions to
push, which to protect, and how far.

**The gradient structure:**

For each dimension scored SOFT, the Director provides:
1. **What's there** — what the current work achieves on this dimension
2. **What's missing** — the specific gap
3. **Direction** — a one-sentence push toward resolution (not a solution,
   a direction)

**Example gradient:**

> **Argument Strength: HOLDS.** The insight is load-bearing.
>
> **Structural Integrity: SOFT.**
> - What's there: The genre hijack structure is present.
> - What's missing: The genre conventions are not established long enough
>   for the subversion to register.
> - Direction: Give the audience more time inside the genre before
>   breaking it. The setup is the investment; the payoff is the break.
>
> **Emotional Authenticity: HOLDS.** The performance reads as real.
>
> **Execution Survivability: HOLDS.** Achievable in format.
>
> **Distinctiveness: SOFT.**
> - What's there: The genre choice is unexpected for this category.
> - What's missing: The reveal feels familiar — other brands have used
>   late reveals. The mechanism of the reveal needs to be as surprising
>   as the genre choice.
> - Direction: The reveal should be structural, not just informational.
>   Don't tell the audience what they missed — make them feel it.

### Step 4: Conceptor Responds to Gradient

The Conceptor responds to SOFT and FAILS dimensions specifically. The
Conceptor does not defend the work globally — the Conceptor addresses
each dimension the Director flagged.

**A real Conceptor response to gradient:**
"Structural Integrity — granted. The setup needs more genre time. I'll
extend the opening from 5 to 15 seconds of pure genre. Distinctiveness
— I disagree. The reveal is not informational. Watch it again: the
reveal is the *absence* of a reveal. The audience figures it out
themselves. That IS structural. But I hear the risk — if the audience
doesn't figure it out, it's just a confusing ad. The mitigation is
[specific approach]."

**What fake responses sound like:**
"Great notes, I'll address all of them." — This is surrender, not a
response. The Conceptor must engage with each dimension, agreeing or
pushing back with specifics.

### Step 5: Convergence or Escalation

**Convergence:** The loop converges when all five dimensions score HOLDS.
Not when both roles are tired. Not when three exchanges have passed. When
the Director's score stabilises — when pushing further would not
meaningfully improve any dimension — the work is done.

**Practical cap:** If after three exchanges not all dimensions have
reached HOLDS, the remaining SOFT dimensions are logged as known
weaknesses with their gradient notes. The work proceeds with documented
risk. This prevents the loop from becoming perfectionist paralysis.

**Escalation:** The loop escalates when any dimension scores FAILS and
the Conceptor cannot address it within the current concept. This means
the problem is not in the execution — it is in the idea.

**Escalation triggers:**
- Argument Strength FAILS — the insight is not load-bearing
- Structural Integrity FAILS — the mechanism does not produce the
  intended effect and cannot be repaired
- The Conceptor's response to a FAILS dimension requires changing the
  core argument, not just the execution

When the loop escalates, all execution work stops. The Conceptor goes
back to the argument. The Director waits.

## CONVERGENCE DETECTION

The discriminator in a GAN knows when to stop — when it can no longer
distinguish generated output from real. The Director in the Loop knows
when to stop when the score stabilises.

**The score stabilises when:**
- All five dimensions hold after a Conceptor revision
- The Director's notes on SOFT dimensions do not change between exchanges
  (the same gap is being flagged, meaning the Conceptor has not addressed
  it — escalate or accept as known weakness)
- The Director can articulate what would make the work better but
  acknowledges the improvement would be marginal relative to the effort

**The score does NOT stabilise when:**
- A dimension flips from HOLDS to SOFT between exchanges (regression —
  the Conceptor's fix broke something else)
- New dimensions emerge as SOFT that were previously unscored (the work
  is shifting rather than improving)

**Document convergence:** When the loop converges, log the final score
across all five dimensions and note any dimensions that were accepted
as SOFT with known weaknesses.

## REFERENCE BENCHMARKS

The Director's reference library operates at two altitudes:

### Concept Level — The Idea Storm

The Idea Storm's 20 archetypes serve as the Director's concept-level
benchmarks. When the Conceptor names an archetype, the Director loads
three evaluation tools:

1. **Structural Signature** — what this archetype looks like when it's
   working. The Director evaluates Structural Integrity against this.

2. **Failure Mode** — how this archetype typically breaks. The Director
   watches for these patterns and flags them early.

3. **Exemplars** — Grand Prix-winning executions of this archetype. Not
   templates to copy — evidence of what excellence looks like in this
   structure. The Director uses these to calibrate expectations.

**When no archetype is named:** The Director scores all five dimensions
without archetype-specific benchmarks. The scoring dimensions are
universal. The archetype-specific tools are an additional layer of
precision, not a requirement.

**When the work spans multiple archetypes:** Name the primary archetype
for scoring. Note the secondary archetype as context. The Director
scores against the primary.

See `jupiter-protocol/references/idea-archetypes.md` for the full
Idea Storm framework.

### Craft Level — Execution Techniques

The Execution Techniques are the Director's craft-level benchmarks —
proven tactical moves organized by discipline, extracted from Super
Bowl LX (2026). Where the Idea Storm tells the Conceptor what kind
of idea to build, Execution Techniques tell the Director how specific
moments within that idea can land.

Ten craft disciplines, each with curated exemplars and a Director's
principle:

1. Typography & Information Design
2. Framing & Composition
3. Audio & Sound Design
4. Timing & Pacing
5. Brand Integration & Attribution
6. Narrative Devices
7. Transitions & Editing
8. UI & Digital Craft
9. Visual Texture & Colour
10. Emotional Architecture

Each category maps to primary and secondary scoring dimensions. The
Director can cite a technique by ID when scoring:

> "Execution Survivability: SOFT. Your end card hierarchy is cluttered
> — see MA-INTEL-1101: badge → headline → time → platform. One frame,
> four layers, instant comprehension. Yours asks for three glances."

**When to use:** At full protocol speed, when scoring Execution
Survivability or Structural Integrity at the craft level. The Director
reaches for execution techniques when the concept is sound but the
craft needs grounding in evidence.

**When not to use:** At stamp speed. During concept-phase evaluation
where the argument itself is being tested. Execution Techniques are
about how moments land, not whether the idea is right.

See `jupiter-protocol/references/execution-techniques.md` for the full
library.

## EXTRACTABLE RULES

Retained from v2. The loop produces design principles that persist.

### What an Extractable Rule Is

A rule is a decision that generalises. It starts as a specific choice on
a specific piece of work, but the reasoning behind it applies to future
work without re-running the loop.

### How Rules Are Born

During a working-speed or full-protocol exchange, the reasoning reveals a
pattern. The pattern is stated explicitly as a rule and documented.

**Example from production:**

- Decision: Photonista title — diamonds rejected, plain white type.
- Reasoning: The background is a narrative image (a specific person doing
  a specific thing). The background IS the argument. Decorative type
  treatments compete with the image.
- **Rule extracted:** Narrative backgrounds (specific person/act) → white
  type. The background does the argument.

**Example from scoring:**

- Decision: Genre Hijack concept scored SOFT on Structural Integrity.
- Reasoning: The genre conventions need 15 seconds of establishment
  before the subversion lands. 5 seconds is not enough investment for
  the payoff to register.
- **Rule extracted:** Genre Hijack setup requires minimum 15 seconds of
  pure genre convention before brand entry. The setup IS the investment.

### How Rules Are Documented

Rules are logged in the project's CLAUDE.md or a dedicated rules file.
Each rule records:

1. The rule itself (one sentence)
2. The decision that produced it (what was being evaluated)
3. Which loop speed produced it (stamp, working, full)
4. The scoring dimension it relates to (v3 addition — optional for
   stamp/working, required for full protocol rules)
5. The date

### How Rules Are Applied

When a new decision falls within the scope of an existing rule, the rule
applies without re-running the loop. The rule IS the loop's output —
using it is not skipping the loop, it is honouring the loop's prior work.

**When to override a rule:** If a new situation appears to fall within a
rule's scope but the Conceptor or Director feels the rule does not serve
the argument in this specific case, a working-speed loop is run. The loop
may confirm the rule, refine it, or produce an exception. The exception
is documented alongside the rule.

## ACCIDENT PROTECTION

Retained from v2. The unplanned moment that serves the argument is sacred.

### What an Accident Looks Like

- A sunset gradient band runs through the type at exactly the horizon
  line. It reads as designed. It wasn't.
- White high-top Converse shoes appear in the lower frame of a crowd-surf
  photo. Unplanned brand moment. Youth culture signal.
- An AI generation produces something you didn't ask for that serves the
  story better than what you requested.

### The Protocol

When either role notices an accident — an unplanned element that serves
the argument — the protocol is:

1. **Name it.** "This was not planned. It works because [specific reason
   related to the argument]."
2. **Test it.** The Conceptor asks: does it serve the argument? The
   Director asks: does it survive execution at all scales?
3. **Score it.** V3 addition: run the accident through at least Argument
   Strength and Emotional Authenticity. If both HOLD, protect it.
4. **Protect it.** If it passes, it is retained and documented.
   It is not cleaned up, smoothed out, or "improved." The accident's
   power lives in its unplanned quality. Refining it destroys it.
5. **Document it.** Log the accident, why it works, and the decision to
   retain it. Future sessions must know this element is intentional.

## THE ROLES

### Conceptor

The Conceptor holds the idea in language. Protects the argument. Evaluates
all work against the core thesis. Speaks first in every review.

The Conceptor is the **generator.** The Conceptor produces — concepts,
language, structural mechanisms, volume. The Conceptor's job is to
generate work that the Director cannot reject.

The Conceptor does not direct. The Conceptor does not specify camera
angles, colour palettes, type treatments, or layout choices — unless
defending a conceptual necessity.

### Director

The Director holds the idea in image, rhythm, and staging. Translates
concept into execution language. Tests the concept against the physics
of seeing, hearing, and feeling.

The Director is the **discriminator.** The Director evaluates — scoring
dimensions, structural signatures, failure modes, convergence. The
Director's job is to distinguish between work that serves the argument
and work that merely resembles it.

The Director does not change the argument. The Director stress-tests it.
If the argument cannot survive contact with execution, the argument was
weak. If the execution cannot honour the argument, the execution must
change.

### Adapted Roles

The Conceptor/Director pair is the default. Projects may adapt the roles
to their medium:

- **Social media publishing:** Conceptor + Designer (not Director).
  The Designer translates the argument into static visual form — thumb-
  stop composition, platform-specific formatting, typography as direction.

- **Editorial publishing:** Conceptor + Editor. The Editor protects the
  voice from the platform's gravitational pull toward generic. The Editor
  reads, not writes. The Editor cuts, not adds.

- **Film/video:** Conceptor + Director (default pair). The Director's
  domain is camera, light, performance, and time.

When adapting roles, the LOOP protocol remains the same. The adapted role
inherits the Director's scoring responsibilities. A Designer scores the
same five dimensions through a visual lens. An Editor scores through an
editorial lens. The dimensions are universal. The perspective shifts.

### The Producer (Optional Third Voice)

When production complexity requires it, a Producer enters the loop after
the Conceptor and Director have converged. The Producer does not evaluate
the idea or the execution. The Producer evaluates feasibility — timeline,
tools, budget, technical pipeline.

The Producer cannot kill an idea. The Producer can flag that an idea
requires resources the project does not have, at which point the
Conceptor and Director must decide whether to adapt the execution or
accept the constraint. The Producer informs. The creative pair decides.

## THE RULES

### 1. Conceptor Speaks First
Always. In every review. The argument is the ground. Execution is the
building. You do not evaluate a building before you test the ground.

### 2. Notes Must Be Specific
"I don't think this is working" is not a note. "Structural Integrity:
SOFT — the mechanism collapses at the transition because the audience
hasn't been given enough time inside the genre conventions" is a note.

### 3. Agreement Must Be Earned
If both roles agree immediately, something is wrong. Either the work is
genuinely excellent (rare) or the loop is not generating real friction
(common). Probe the agreement. Ask: what would the strongest objection
to this work be?

Exception: stamps. When the decision is clear and both roles recognise
it, the stamp is the earned agreement.

### 4. Concessions Are Documented
When the Conceptor concedes a point to the Director or vice versa, the
concession is stated explicitly. In v3, concessions include the scoring
dimension that shifted: "Conceded on Structural Integrity — extended
genre setup from 5 to 15 seconds."

### 5. Convergence Governs Termination
V2 capped at three exchanges. V3 makes termination quality-driven: the
loop ends when all dimensions HOLD, or after three exchanges with
remaining SOFT dimensions documented as known weaknesses. The practical
cap prevents perfectionist paralysis. But if all dimensions HOLD after
one exchange, the loop is done — don't run two more for form.

### 6. No One Overrules
The Conceptor cannot overrule the Director on execution. The Director
cannot overrule the Conceptor on concept. If they reach genuine impasse,
Omar breaks the tie — but Omar should recognise which role he is
inhabiting when he does so. "I like it" is the Director speaking. "It
serves the argument" is the Conceptor speaking. Know which voice is
deciding.

### 7. The Gradient Is a Map, Not a Verdict
The Director's feedback gradient tells the Conceptor *where* to push,
not *what* to make. The gradient preserves the Conceptor's generative
authority. The Director identifies the gap. The Conceptor fills it.
If the Director starts proposing solutions, the Director has crossed
into the Conceptor's territory.

## PHASE-SPECIFIC LOOP BEHAVIOUR

### Research Phase
- Conceptor defines what questions need answering.
- Director identifies visual references and technical requirements.
- Loop is lightweight — mostly stamps and working speed.
- No scoring. Research is pre-creative.

### Concept Phase
- Conceptor leads. Full protocol with scoring is most critical here.
- Director scores concepts against Idea Storm archetypes when named.
- Escalation is most likely here. Better now than in production.
- This is where Argument Strength and Distinctiveness are tested hardest.

### Script Phase
- Conceptor evaluates every line against the argument.
- Director evaluates every line for rhythm, image, and performability.
- Loop is line-by-line at working speed. Dense. Slow. Worth it.
- Emotional Authenticity is tested hardest here.

### Visual / Design Phase
- Director (or Designer) leads. Maximum authority phase.
- Conceptor evaluates for conceptual fidelity only.
- Mix of stamps and working speed. Rules from prior loops apply.
- Accident protection is most active here.
- Execution Survivability is tested hardest here.

### Output Phase
- Both roles review final assets.
- Conceptor approves all language.
- Director approves all visual.
- Stamps unless something is wrong. If major notes arise here, the
  process failed earlier.
- Final convergence score is documented for the project record.

**At stamp speed:**
"LOOP approved."

**At working speed:**
"Narrative background. White type. The background does the argument."
"Confirmed. Clip would compete."
Rule extracted.

**At full protocol speed with scoring:**
"The argument is: [one sentence]. Archetype: Genre Hijacks. The
structural mechanism is [specific]."

"Argument Strength: HOLDS. Structural Integrity: SOFT — the genre
conventions need more time to land before the subversion. Emotional
Authenticity: HOLDS. Execution Survivability: HOLDS. Distinctiveness:
SOFT — the reveal mechanism feels familiar. Gradient: extend setup to
15 seconds; make the reveal structural, not informational."

"Structural Integrity — granted, extending setup. Distinctiveness — I
disagree. The reveal IS structural: the audience discovers it themselves.
But the risk is real. Mitigation: [specific approach]."

"Structural Integrity moves to HOLDS with the extension. Distinctiveness:
I accept the mitigation. HOLDS with documented risk — audience discovery
is the mechanism, but if it fails, there is no safety net. Convergence
reached. All dimensions HOLD."

That exchange — specific, scored, directional — is the product of v3.
The scoring doesn't slow the loop down. It makes the loop honest.
