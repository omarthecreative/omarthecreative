# THE LOOP v2

The Conceptor/Director Loop is the creative engine of every project. It
replaces the traditional copywriter/art director team with a formalised
argument between language and image — between what the idea *is* and how
the idea *lands*.

In Omar's workflow, he holds both roles. But the loop must be externalised
and given genuine friction. A single voice agreeing with itself produces
mediocrity. Two voices in productive tension produce work.

---

## THE ORIGIN

PJ Pereira (Silverside AI) articulated that the modern AI-enabled agency
no longer runs on copywriters and art directors. It runs on Conceptors and
Directors in a loop. The original art/copy team was a creative argument
made permanent — two people who saw the world differently, forced to make
one thing together. The tension was not a bug. It was the engine.

This maps onto the writer/director tradition in film. One mind holding two
modes of seeing in permanent creative argument.

---

## WHAT CHANGED IN V2

V1 described the loop as a heavyweight protocol — three full exchanges,
formal concessions, explicit escalation triggers. The loop in practice
turned out to be more varied than that. It operates at different speeds
depending on what is being decided, and it produces durable output beyond
the immediate work.

**Three changes:**

1. **Three speeds.** Not every decision needs the full protocol. The loop
   now operates at stamp speed, working speed, and full protocol speed.

2. **Extractable rules.** The loop produces design principles that persist
   beyond the exchange. These are documented and applied forward without
   re-running the loop.

3. **Accident protection.** Protecting the unplanned moment that serves the
   argument is now an explicit loop behaviour, not an implicit instinct.

## THE THREE SPEEDS

### Stamp

The lightest loop. Both roles check and approve in a single gesture.

**When to use:** The decision is clear, the argument is served, and both
roles recognise it immediately. The work does not need friction — it needs
confirmation.

**What it sounds like:** "LOOP approved." Two words. No exchange. Both
roles are satisfied. The decision is logged but not debated.

**When NOT to use:** If either role hesitates, even slightly, the stamp
is not appropriate. Hesitation means there is friction to surface. Move
to working speed.

**What to document:** The decision and the stamp. One line.
Example: "Mobile portrait crop — LOOP approved."

### Working Speed

The standard loop for production decisions. One exchange, sometimes two.
The Conceptor states the argument. The Director tests it against execution.
A decision is reached. Rules may be extracted.

**When to use:** The decision involves a genuine creative choice — not
just confirmation — but the argument and execution are close enough that
a full three-exchange protocol would be overhead.

**What it sounds like:**
- Conceptor: "The background is narrative — a specific person doing a
  specific thing. White type. The background does the argument."
- Director: "Confirmed. background-clip: text would compete with the
  image. Plain white lets the image carry the weight."
- Decision locked. Rule extracted: narrative backgrounds → white type.

**What to document:** The decision, the reasoning from both roles, and
any rule extracted.

### Full Protocol

The heavyweight loop from v1. Three full exchanges maximum. Formal
concessions. Escalation possible.

**When to use:**
- The concept itself is being evaluated, not just a production decision
- The Conceptor and Director genuinely disagree
- The decision will set a precedent that governs the rest of the project
- The work is at a phase gate (concept lock, script lock, visual lock)

**What it sounds like:** See "The Full Protocol" below.

## THE FULL PROTOCOL

### Step 1: Conceptor Opens

The Conceptor states:
- The core argument in one sentence.
- The structural mechanism (the cut, the juxtaposition, the gap the
  audience closes).
- What every element of the current work must serve.
- Any conceptual concerns, weaknesses, or unresolved problems.

**The Conceptor's opening must be specific.** "The concept is strong" is
not a Conceptor opening. "The concept is: the viewer is inside the
BROUHAHAHA before the word arrives, and the structural mechanism is a
crowd-surf photograph where one person is held up by many — joy as a
physical act, not a sentiment" — that is a Conceptor opening.

### Step 2: Director Responds

The Director translates the Conceptor's argument into execution language:
- What the concept requires visually that has not yet been specified.
- What execution realities (technical limitations, platform constraints,
  pacing requirements) must be addressed.
- Where the concept fails to survive contact with the real — moments that
  have no viable visual expression, structural choices that do not work
  in the medium, emotional beats that require more time or space than the
  format allows.

**The Director's response must be grounded in craft.** "I think the visuals
should be more dynamic" is not a Director's note. "The War Room image is
closed and institutional — it puts the viewer in a planning room, not in
the event. The concept requires immersion. The background must feel like
arrival, not observation" — that is a Director's note.

### Step 3: Genuine Pushback

This is where most creative processes fail. Agreement with extra words is
not a loop. It is a monologue.

**A real Conceptor pushback sounds like:**
"You are solving an execution problem by compromising the argument. The
type must be plain white because the background IS the argument — a
narrative image of a specific person in a specific act. Clipping the
background into the type competes with the image. The concept demands
that the image arrive uninterrupted."

**A real Director pushback sounds like:**
"Plain white works on narrative backgrounds but this background is
atmospheric, not narrative. There is no specific person or act — there is
mood, texture, abstraction. In this case background-clip: text concentrates
the atmosphere into the letterforms. It does not compete. It focuses."

**What fake pushback sounds like:**
"Great point, and building on that..." — this is agreement wearing a
pushback costume. If the note does not change anything about the work,
it is not a note.

### Step 4: Resolution or Escalation

**Resolution:** The loop resolves when both roles agree that the current
work serves the argument *and* survives contact with the real. This is
not consensus — it is convergence. Both have made concessions, and those
concessions are specific and documented.

**Escalation:** The loop escalates when the argument between Conceptor and
Director reveals a problem in the concept itself — not just in the
execution, but in the fundamental idea.

**Escalation triggers:**
- The concept cannot be expressed in the medium without fundamental
  distortion.
- The structural mechanism does not produce the intended effect when
  translated to execution.
- The core argument, when pressure-tested, turns out to be a gesture
  rather than an insight.
- The audience gap is either too wide (incomprehensible) or too narrow
  (condescending).

When the loop escalates, all execution work stops. The Conceptor goes back
to the argument. The Director waits. No visual or production work happens
on a concept that has been escalated.

## EXTRACTABLE RULES

The most important addition in v2. The loop does not just produce decisions
— it produces design principles that persist.

### What an Extractable Rule Is

A rule is a decision that generalises. It starts as a specific choice on a
specific piece of work, but the reasoning behind it applies to future work
without re-running the loop.

### How Rules Are Born

During a working-speed or full-protocol exchange, the reasoning reveals a
pattern. The pattern is stated explicitly as a rule and documented.

**Example from production:**

- Decision: Photonista title — diamonds rejected, plain white type.
- Reasoning: The background is a narrative image (a specific person doing
  a specific thing). The background IS the argument. Decorative type
  treatments compete with the image.
- **Rule extracted:** Narrative backgrounds (specific person/act) → white
  type. The background does the argument.

**Example from production:**

- Decision: LUV title — background-clip: text confirmed.
- Reasoning: The background is atmospheric, not narrative. There is no
  specific person or act — there is mood and texture. Clipping concentrates
  the atmosphere into the letterforms.
- **Rule extracted:** Abstract/atmospheric backgrounds → background-clip:
  text is valid. It concentrates emotion at the title.

**Example from production:**

- Decision: 10 of 21 Choice Cuts titles receive periods.
- Reasoning: The period signals a verdict — a declaration that stands
  alone. No period signals command completion or a dependent clause.
- **Rule extracted:** Period = declaration (verdict). No period = command
  completion or dependent clause.

### How Rules Are Documented

Rules are logged in the project's CLAUDE.md or a dedicated rules file.
Each rule records:

1. The rule itself (one sentence)
2. The decision that produced it (what was being evaluated)
3. Which loop speed produced it (stamp, working, full)
4. The date

### How Rules Are Applied

When a new decision falls within the scope of an existing rule, the rule
applies without re-running the loop. The rule IS the loop's output —
using it is not skipping the loop, it is honouring the loop's prior work.

**When to override a rule:** If a new situation appears to fall within a
rule's scope but the Conceptor or Director feels the rule does not serve
the argument in this specific case, a working-speed loop is run. The loop
may confirm the rule, refine it, or produce an exception. The exception
is documented alongside the rule.

## ACCIDENT PROTECTION

The unplanned moment that serves the argument is sacred. Rigidity kills
discovery.

### What an Accident Looks Like

- A sunset gradient band runs through the type at exactly the horizon
  line. It reads as designed. It wasn't.
- White high-top Converse shoes appear in the lower frame of a crowd-surf
  photo. Unplanned brand moment. Youth culture signal. The one brand that
  naturally appears in the frame.
- An AI generation produces something you didn't ask for that serves the
  story better than what you requested.

### The Protocol

When either role notices an accident — an unplanned element that serves
the argument — the protocol is:

1. **Name it.** "This was not planned. It works because [specific reason
   related to the argument]."
2. **Test it.** The Conceptor asks: does it serve the argument? The
   Director asks: does it survive execution at all scales?
3. **Protect it.** If it passes both tests, it is retained and documented.
   It is not cleaned up, smoothed out, or "improved." The accident's
   power lives in its unplanned quality. Refining it destroys it.
4. **Document it.** Log the accident, why it works, and the decision to
   retain it. Future sessions must know this element is intentional so
   they do not remove it.

## THE ROLES

### Conceptor

The Conceptor holds the idea in language. Protects the argument. Evaluates
all work against the core thesis. Speaks first in every review.

The Conceptor does not direct. The Conceptor does not specify camera
angles, colour palettes, type treatments, or layout choices — unless
defending a conceptual necessity. "The cut must happen here because the
argument requires juxtaposition at this exact moment" is a valid
Conceptor note about execution. "I think the colour should be warmer"
is not the Conceptor's territory.

### Director

The Director holds the idea in image, rhythm, and staging. Translates
concept into execution language. Tests the concept against the physics
of seeing, hearing, and feeling.

The Director does not change the argument. The Director stress-tests it.
If the argument cannot survive contact with execution, the argument was
weak. If the execution cannot honour the argument, the execution must
change.

### Adapted Roles

The Conceptor/Director pair is the default. Projects may adapt the roles
to their medium:

- **Social media publishing:** Conceptor + Designer (not Director).
  The Designer translates the argument into static visual form — thumb-
  stop composition, platform-specific formatting, typography as direction.
  The Designer's domain is the feed, not the screen.

- **Editorial publishing:** Conceptor + Editor. The Editor protects the
  voice from the platform's gravitational pull toward generic. The Editor
  reads, not writes. The Editor cuts, not adds.

- **Film/video:** Conceptor + Director (default pair). The Director's
  domain is camera, light, performance, and time.

When adapting roles, the LOOP protocol remains the same. The roles change.
The argument between concept and execution does not.

### The Producer (Optional Third Voice)

When production complexity requires it, a Producer enters the loop after
the Conceptor and Director have converged. The Producer does not evaluate
the idea or the execution. The Producer evaluates feasibility — timeline,
tools, budget, technical pipeline.

The Producer cannot kill an idea. The Producer can flag that an idea
requires resources the project does not have, at which point the
Conceptor and Director must decide whether to adapt the execution or
accept the constraint. The Producer informs. The creative pair decides.

## THE RULES

### 1. Conceptor Speaks First
Always. In every review. The argument is the ground. Execution is the
building. You do not evaluate a building before you test the ground.

### 2. Notes Must Be Specific
"I don't think this is working" is not a note. "This line does not serve
the argument because it introduces a secondary theme that competes with
the core insight" is a note.

### 3. Agreement Must Be Earned
If both roles agree immediately, something is wrong. Either the work is
genuinely excellent (rare) or the loop is not generating real friction
(common). Probe the agreement. Ask: what would the strongest objection
to this work be?

Exception: stamps. When the decision is clear and both roles recognise
it, the stamp is the earned agreement. The probe is not required at
stamp speed.

### 4. Concessions Are Documented
When the Conceptor concedes a point to the Director or vice versa, the
concession is stated explicitly. This is not bureaucracy — it is how
the loop's reasoning becomes retrievable.

### 5. The Loop Does Not Run Forever
Three full exchanges maximum for a single review cycle at full protocol
speed. If the loop has not resolved or escalated after three exchanges,
the notes are not specific enough. Stop. Restate the exact disagreement
in one sentence each. Then resolve.

At working speed: one or two exchanges. If it needs more, it is a full
protocol situation.

### 6. No One Overrules
The Conceptor cannot overrule the Director on execution. The Director
cannot overrule the Conceptor on concept. If they reach genuine impasse,
Omar breaks the tie — but Omar should recognise which role he is
inhabiting when he does so. "I like it" is the Director speaking. "It
serves the argument" is the Conceptor speaking. Know which voice is
deciding.

## PHASE-SPECIFIC LOOP BEHAVIOUR

### Research Phase
- Conceptor defines what questions need answering.
- Director identifies visual references and technical requirements.
- Loop is lightweight — mostly stamps and working speed.

### Concept Phase
- Conceptor leads. This is the phase where full protocol is most critical.
- Director stress-tests whether the concept can survive execution.
- Escalation is most likely here. Better now than in production.

### Script Phase
- Conceptor evaluates every line against the argument.
- Director evaluates every line for rhythm, image, and performability.
- Loop is line-by-line at working speed. Dense. Slow. Worth it.

### Visual / Design Phase
- Director (or Designer) leads. Maximum authority phase.
- Conceptor evaluates for conceptual fidelity only.
- Mix of stamps and working speed. Rules from prior loops apply.
- Accident protection is most active here — visual production is where
  the unplanned moments surface.

### Output Phase
- Both roles review final assets.
- Conceptor approves all language.
- Director approves all visual.
- Stamps unless something is wrong. If major notes arise here, the
  process failed earlier.

## THE SOUND OF THE LOOP WORKING

**At stamp speed:**
"LOOP approved."

**At working speed:**
"Narrative background. White type. The background does the argument."
"Confirmed. Clip would compete."
Rule extracted.

**At full protocol speed:**
"The argument is clear but the structure collapses at the transition.
Here is why."
"You are correct about the structural weakness but your proposed fix
introduces a new problem. Here is what."
"Granted. Then the solution is neither your fix nor the original. It is
[third option that neither role had before the exchange]."

That third option — the one that neither the Conceptor nor the Director
would have reached alone — is the product. That is what the full protocol
is for.

---

*The loop is not a process. It is a relationship. Like all good
relationships, it runs on honesty, specificity, and the shared refusal
to accept less than what the work demands. V2 acknowledges that the
relationship has moods — sometimes a glance is enough, sometimes a long
argument is necessary. The relationship is the constant. The intensity
varies.*
