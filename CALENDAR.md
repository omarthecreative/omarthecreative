## THE EDITORIAL PRINCIPLE

The less crowded the moment, the higher the priority.

Everyone posts on International Women's Day. Almost nobody posts on Daylight Saving Time. The two people who post on Daylight Saving Time own it. Besos and Disheveled should be scanning for the second category: universally felt, editorially abandoned.

Crowdedness is not disqualifying — IWD and Mother's Day are worth owning if the angle is sharp enough to cut through the noise. But a low-crowdedness moment with a good angle beats a high-crowdedness moment with a great angle, because attention is finite and competition is the enemy of impact.

---

## CROWDEDNESS KEY

- **HIGH** — Everyone posts. The feed is saturated. You need an exceptional angle to justify entry.
- **MEDIUM** — Some people post. There is room, but you are not alone.
- **LOW** — Almost nobody posts. The moment is universally felt but editorially abandoned. Own it.

---

## CASE STUDIES

### IWD — March 8 (HIGH crowdedness)
The founding Besos post was triggered by IWD. It worked because it did what nobody else does on IWD: it was personal before it was institutional. It named specific women. It led with loss. It offered something concrete. The angle that cuts through a crowded moment is always the one that is irreducibly specific to the person posting. Generic solidarity dies in a crowded feed.

### Daylight Saving Time — March 8, 2026 (LOW crowdedness)
Everyone loses an hour. Nobody posts about it with any depth. The angle: time is the only resource you cannot manufacture. Or: the collective agreement to pretend an hour doesn't exist. Or: a Disheveled lesson on time perception from a book on the shelf. The low crowdedness means even a good-not-great post owns the moment.

## RECURRING WEEKLY MOMENTS

These are not calendar dates. They are rhythmic opportunities:

- **Monday morning** — the collective dread. The angle: reframing the start.
- **Friday afternoon** — the collective exhale. The angle: what the week taught you.
- **Sunday evening** — the transition. The angle: preparation as creative act.

## UPDATE PROTOCOL

This calendar should be refreshed weekly. Sources to monitor:

1. Global observance calendars (UN, UNESCO, WHO)
2. US federal and cultural calendars
3. Advertising industry calendars (Cannes, D&AD, One Show, Clios)
4. Latino and Latin American cultural calendars
5. Literary and publishing calendars
6. News cycles — breaking moments that create unexpected editorial openings
7. Seasonal shifts — solstice, equinox, weather events, time changes

The best moments are the ones nobody else thought to claim. Scan for those first. The crowded moments are backup.
